# Product And Business Strategy

## Market Positioning

LatticeOps should position itself as the enterprise operations layer for governed AI workforces.

Core message:

AI agents are moving into real business work. LatticeOps gives organizations the control plane to deploy them safely: graph-native delegation, secure execution, artifact governance, human approvals, and complete audit trails.

## Target Market

Initial market:

- Mid-market and enterprise engineering organizations experimenting with agentic software work.
- AI platform teams tasked with standardizing agent deployments.
- Regulated operations teams that need audit and human review.

Expansion market:

- Customer support automation.
- Security operations.
- Financial operations.
- Legal and compliance operations.
- Government and public sector.

## Competitive Strategy

Compete against:

- Developer frameworks by offering production operations and governance.
- Automation platforms by offering graph-native AI workers and artifact-first execution.
- LLM observability tools by offering execution, policy, and artifact governance, not just traces.
- Internal platforms by reducing time and risk to build the same foundation.

Differentiators:

- Human and AI actors in one accountable graph.
- Artifact-centered work rather than chat-centered output.
- Built-in policy, approvals, runtime isolation, and audit.
- Cloud and self-host deployment.
- Strong package and graph versioning.

## Growth Strategy

Phase 1: Technical credibility.

- Publish architecture content.
- Provide open-source SDKs and local developer edition.
- Build templates for engineering, research, and support workflows.
- Win design partners through depth, not broad marketing.

Phase 2: Team adoption.

- Make onboarding fast.
- Provide workspace templates.
- Add GitHub, Slack, Jira, and document integrations.
- Build visible ROI dashboards.

Phase 3: Enterprise expansion.

- Add SSO, SCIM, audit exports, policy packs, self-host, and compliance support.
- Sell to AI platform, security, and operations leadership.

## Pricing

Suggested pricing model:

- Free developer edition: local or limited cloud usage.
- Team plan: per seat plus included active workers and executions.
- Business plan: higher limits, SSO, integrations, advanced observability.
- Enterprise plan: self-host, SCIM, custom retention, audit exports, dedicated support, private deployment.

Usage dimensions:

- Seats.
- Active workers.
- Session executions.
- Artifact storage.
- Trace and audit retention.
- Model gateway pass-through or bring-your-own-key.

Pricing principles:

- Avoid surprising customers with hidden model markups.
- Provide budgets and forecasts.
- Align price to governed work value, not only API calls.

## Retention Strategy

Retention levers:

- Artifact history becomes institutional memory.
- Graphs become operational assets.
- Integrations embed the product in daily workflows.
- Audit and compliance evidence create switching costs.
- Templates and package registry compound over time.

Risks to retention:

- Agents produce low-quality outputs.
- Setup is too complex.
- Security teams block adoption.
- Costs are unpredictable.
- Debugging failures is difficult.

Mitigation:

- Strong onboarding.
- Evaluations and regression testing.
- Cost controls.
- Approval and review workflows.
- Clear traceability.

## Marketing Narrative

Avoid claiming fully autonomous business replacement. The stronger and safer narrative is:

- Governed AI delegation.
- Human accountable operations.
- Durable artifacts.
- Evidence and audit.
- Production readiness.

## Sales Motion

Land:

- AI platform team or engineering automation use case.
- Demonstrate first graph running with GitHub, Slack, and artifact review.

Expand:

- Add departments, environments, integrations, and policy packs.
- Use analytics to show cost savings, cycle-time reduction, and controlled risk.

Enterprise close:

- SSO, audit, self-host, support, and compliance readiness.
